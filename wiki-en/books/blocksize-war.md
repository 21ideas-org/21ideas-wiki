## Summary

The definitive account of the 2015–2017 conflict over Bitcoin's block size limit. Bier is a Bitcoin researcher who documented the conflict in real time; this book is a chronological, detailed history of every major event in the war. Available also as an audiobook.

The [[en/history/blocksize-war|blocksize war]] is the most important [[en/concepts/governance|governance]] event in Bitcoin's history: it determined whether [[en/concepts/mining|miners]] or [[en/concepts/bitcoin-node|full node]] operators controlled Bitcoin's rules. Small blockers won.

## Key Takeaways

**Miners don't control Bitcoin**: Despite controlling [[en/concepts/mining|hash rate]] and producing blocks, miners could not force a [[en/concepts/forks|hard fork]]. [[en/concepts/bitcoin-node|Full node]] operators and the economic majority determined the outcome.

**ASICBoost explains everything**: Bitmain's covert [[en/concepts/mining|mining]] optimization was incompatible with [[en/concepts/segwit|SegWit]]. Their opposition to SegWit was not principled — it was economic self-interest. Once exposed, their credibility collapsed.

**UASF was the decisive move**: The threat of [[en/concepts/bitcoin-node|full node]] operators enforcing [[en/concepts/segwit|SegWit]] regardless of miner signaling changed the game. Miners faced losing all their revenue if they mined blocks the economic majority rejected.

**Craig Wright's involvement**: His fraudulent Satoshi claim and alignment with large blockers damaged that side's credibility. The book documents his behavior in detail.

**The New York Agreement collapsed**: 58 companies signed, but without [[en/concepts/bitcoin-core|Bitcoin Core]] developers, without [[en/concepts/bitcoin-node|full node]] operators, and without the economic majority, the agreement was meaningless. [[en/concepts/segwit|SegWit2x]] was cancelled by email to signatories, essentially admitting they had overestimated their power.

## Why It Matters Today

The blocksize war set the template for Bitcoin [[en/concepts/governance|governance]]: [[en/concepts/forks|soft forks]] are the path of least resistance; [[en/concepts/forks|hard forks]] require genuine network-wide consensus; [[en/concepts/bitcoin-node|full nodes]] are supreme. This template is now part of Bitcoin culture. Any future attempts to change Bitcoin's fundamental rules will face the same resistance — including attempts to raise the [[en/concepts/scarcity|21M cap]].
